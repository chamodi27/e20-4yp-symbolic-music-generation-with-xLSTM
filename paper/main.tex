% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}
Generating long-form symbolic music with coherent structure remains a significant challenge in AI music generation. While Transformer-based approaches have made notable progress --- with models like Museformer introducing specialized attention mechanisms for long music sequences --- the dominant paradigm still relies on attention-based architectures. In this work, we explore the Extended Long Short-Term Memory (xLSTM) architecture as an alternative for symbolic music generation, leveraging its recurrent state formulation to achieve efficient, linear-time generation of arbitrarily long sequences with constant memory usage. We train an xLSTM model on the Lakh MIDI Dataset using the REMIGEN encoding and evaluate its generation capabilities across sequence lengths from 2,048 to 12,288 tokens. A key practical contribution is the implementation of a recurrent-state inference method that correctly utilizes xLSTM's intended generation mode, achieving up to 25$\times$ speedup over a naive parallel-mode baseline. We analyze the generated output in terms of musical properties, generation performance, and token grammar adherence. We further analyze the generated output using objective musical metrics and human listening tests, which indicate meaningful repetition patterns, musically plausible note distributions, and perceptible long-term structural coherence. Our results demonstrate that xLSTM is a viable architecture for long-form symbolic music generation, offering a fundamentally different trade-off compared to Transformer-based approaches.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Automatic music generation has been a long-standing goal in artificial intelligence. Symbolic music generation --- which operates on discrete representations such as MIDI --- has received considerable attention in recent years \cite{chen2024survey}. A central challenge in this domain is generating pieces that are not only locally coherent (sounding pleasant at any given moment) but also exhibit long-term structure: recurring themes, consistent instrumentation, and musically meaningful form over hundreds of bars.

Transformer-based models have become the dominant architecture for sequence modeling tasks, including symbolic music generation. However, standard full attention has quadratic complexity with respect to sequence length, which limits its direct applicability to long music sequences that can span tens of thousands of tokens. Several works have proposed solutions within the Transformer framework. Most notably, Museformer \cite{yu2022museformer} introduces a fine- and coarse-grained attention mechanism that selectively attends to structure-related bars at full resolution while summarizing others. This approach achieves effective complexity between linear and quadratic, enabling generation of full-length songs. Such approaches demonstrate that long-form music generation is achievable with Transformers, but they require careful architectural design to manage the cost of attention.

The Extended Long Short-Term Memory (xLSTM) architecture \cite{beck2024xlstm} offers a fundamentally different approach. As a modern recurrent architecture with exponential gating and matrix memory, xLSTM supports a dual formulation: a parallel mode for efficient training and a recurrent mode for inference. In recurrent mode, the model maintains a fixed-size state that compresses the entire sequence history, enabling constant-time and constant-memory generation per token regardless of sequence length. This property makes xLSTM a natural candidate for long-form music generation, where models must maintain context over thousands of tokens.

In this paper, we investigate the application of xLSTM to symbolic music generation, with a focus on the following contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item We train an xLSTM model on the Lakh MIDI Dataset (LMD) using the REMIGEN encoding scheme and the Helibrunna training framework, and report training dynamics and model selection via perplexity analysis.
    \item We identify and resolve a critical inference bottleneck in the Helibrunna framework by implementing a recurrent-state generator that achieves linear-time generation with constant memory, yielding up to 25$\times$ speedup over the original implementation.
    \item We evaluate the generation process across multiple sequence lengths (2,048 to 12,288 tokens), measuring musical output properties, generation performance, and token grammar quality.

\subsection{xLSTM and Modern Recurrent Architectures}
The xLSTM architecture \cite{beck2024xlstm} revisits and extends the original LSTM with two key innovations: the scalar LSTM (sLSTM) with exponential gating and memory mixing, and the matrix LSTM (mLSTM) with a matrix-valued memory and a covariance update rule. A critical feature of xLSTM is its support for dual formulations --- a parallel mode that processes all tokens simultaneously for efficient GPU training, and a recurrent mode that processes tokens one at a time with a fixed-size state for efficient inference. Both formulations produce mathematically identical outputs for the same input.

The xLSTM architecture has been applied successfully in various domains including time series forecasting \cite{alharthi2024xlstmtime}, image segmentation \cite{dutta2024vision}, and audio representation learning \cite{yadav2025axlstm}. However, to the best of our knowledge, its application to symbolic music generation --- particularly exploiting its recurrent formulation for long-form generation --- has not been previously explored.

The REMIGEN encoding converts MIDI files into flat sequences of discrete tokens, each using a prefix-value format as summarized in Table~\ref{tab:remigen_tokens}. Some tokens capture global properties of the piece (time signature, tempo), while others mark structural boundaries (bar boundary) or specify individual notes. Each note is represented as a triplet of three consecutive tokens --- pitch, duration, and velocity (p-d-v) --- forming a strict grammar that the model must learn to follow. This textual representation allows the music generation problem to be framed as a standard language modeling task \cite{yu2022museformer}.

\subsection{Dataset}
\label{sec:dataset}

\subsubsection{Source Data}
We use the Lakh MIDI Dataset (LMD) \cite{raffel2016lmd}, a large-scale collection of MIDI files widely used in music information retrieval and generation research. Museformer \cite{yu2022museformer} also uses LMD as its training corpus, making it a natural choice for comparison.

\subsubsection{Pre-processing}
\label{sec:preprocessing}

We follow the MuseFormer preprocessing methodology~\cite{yu2022museformer} to obtain a
clean and structurally consistent MIDI corpus from the LMD.
Starting from the 29,940-file curated list provided by MuseFormer (itself a filtered
subset of the full LMD), we apply a five-stage pipeline that produces
\textbf{24,402 MIDI files} ready for tokenisation.
Our dataset is therefore a strict subset of the MuseFormer split, obtained by
independently re-executing the same preprocessing rules.

\paragraph{Stage~1 --- MIDI Parsing.}
Every candidate MIDI file is parsed using the \texttt{miditoolkit} Python library.
Per-file metadata (time signatures, tempo range, pitch range, note count, track count,
and empty-bar statistics) is recorded in a manifest CSV that drives all downstream
stages. Files that fail to parse are discarded.

\paragraph{Stage~2 --- Melody Track Detection.}
We use \texttt{midi-miner}~\cite{midiminer}, a machine-learning-based track-role
classifier, to assign roles (\emph{melody}, \emph{bass}, \emph{drum}, or
\emph{other}) to each instrument track.
Files for which no melody track is detected are excluded.

\paragraph{Stage~3 --- Instrument Compression to Six Tracks.}
Following MuseFormer, each file is reduced to exactly six canonical instrument
channels: \texttt{square\_synth} (melody, GM~program~80),
\texttt{piano} (programs~0--7), \texttt{guitar} (programs~24--31),
\texttt{string} (programs~40--51, 88--95), \texttt{bass} (programs~32--39),
and \texttt{drum} (percussion channel).
When multiple source tracks compete for the same target channel, the top-$K$ tracks
by note count are merged ($K{=}2$).
Per-track cleanup is applied: melody and bass are enforced to be monophonic; piano,
guitar, and string polyphony is capped (piano~$\leq 10$ simultaneous notes;
guitar/string~$\leq 6$); and exact duplicate notes are removed.

\paragraph{Stage~4 --- MIDI Normalisation.}
Each compressed MIDI file is routed through MuseScore Studio~4~\cite{musescore}
in headless mode to quantise note timings to a consistent grid, enforce a uniform
tempo and time-signature representation, and resolve common encoding edge cases
(e.g.,~overlapping notes and sustain-pedal artefacts).

\paragraph{Stage~5 --- Quality Filtering and Pitch Normalisation.}
We apply the filtering rules from MuseFormer~\cite{yu2022museformer} to remove
low-quality and musically degenerate content; these rules are summarised in
Table~\ref{tab:filter_rules}.
Following filtering, all surviving files are transposed to a canonical key using the
Krumhansl--Kessler pitch-class correlation method~\cite{krumhansl1990cognitive}:
major-key pieces are transposed to C~major and minor-key pieces to A~minor, with the
shift constrained to $\pm 6$~semitones and octave adjustments applied as needed to
keep all notes within MIDI~21--108 (A0--C8).

\subsection{Model Architecture and Training}
\label{sec:training}

\subsubsection{Architecture Configuration}
We use the xLSTM architecture \cite{beck2024xlstm} with the configuration detailed in Table~\ref{tab:model_config}. The model consists of 12 blocks with an embedding dimension of 512 and a context length of 4,096 tokens. The architecture uses a mix of mLSTM and sLSTM blocks: sLSTM blocks are placed at layers 3, 6, and 9, while the remaining layers use mLSTM blocks. This follows the design principle described in the xLSTM paper, where sLSTM provides state-tracking capabilities through memory mixing, while mLSTM provides larger memory capacity through its matrix-valued memory.

\subsection{Recurrent Inference for Efficient Generation}
\label{sec:recurrent_inference}

\subsubsection{The Inference Bottleneck}
While the xLSTM model trained successfully using Helibrunna, we encountered severe performance issues during the generation phase. Generation times grew rapidly with sequence length, and attempts to generate longer sequences resulted in out-of-memory (OOM) errors, making it infeasible to generate full-length musical pieces.

\subsubsection{Root Cause}
The performance issue was not a limitation of the xLSTM architecture itself. The xLSTM supports two mathematically equivalent formulations: a parallel mode (used during training, processes all tokens at once) and a recurrent mode (intended for generation, processes one token at a time with a fixed-size state). The recurrent mode is far more efficient for autoregressive generation, offering $O(1)$ cost per token and constant memory usage. However, Helibrunna's generation loop was using the parallel mode --- reprocessing the entire sequence from scratch at every step instead of the more efficient recurrent mode.

\subsubsection{Solution: Recurrent-State Generator}
We implemented a custom generator that bypasses Helibrunna's generation loop and directly uses the xLSTM model's native recurrent inference mode. The generator first processes the prompt tokens sequentially to build an initial recurrent state. Then, at each generation step, it passes only the most recently generated token and the current state to the model, which returns the next-token logits and an updated state. Temperature scaling is applied and the next token is sampled from the resulting distribution.

No model weights, architecture, tokenizer, or training procedures were modified. The improvement comes entirely from using the model's intended inference mode.

\subsubsection{Validation of Equivalence}
To verify that the recurrent generator produces identical outputs to the original parallel-mode generator, we conducted a controlled equivalence test. Both generators were initialized with the same checkpoint and random seed, and asked to generate 100 tokens from the same prompt. The outputs were identical character-by-character, confirming mathematical equivalence.

\subsection{Grammar-Aware Token Filtering}
\label{sec:grammar_filtering}

The REMIGEN encoding enforces a strict grammar in which each note must be represented as a pitch-duration-velocity (p-d-v) triplet. During autoregressive generation, the model occasionally produces tokens that violate this grammar --- for example, generating a pitch token without an immediately following duration and velocity token. Such violations cause the REMIGEN decoder to fail.

To address this, we apply a post-generation token repair step before MIDI decoding. After the full token sequence is generated, we first attempt direct decoding. If that fails, a repair pass scans the raw token sequence and removes any incomplete or orphaned p-d-v tokens (while preserving all other structural tokens such as bar, position, time signature, and tempo tokens), producing a grammatically valid sequence. Grammar errors are always measured on the original, unrepaired token stream to reflect the model's true output quality. The impact of grammar violations on output quality is evaluated in Section~\ref{sec:results_quality}.

\subsection{Evaluation of the Generation Process}
\label{sec:eval_methodology}

To evaluate how the xLSTM model's generation behaves across different sequence lengths, we generated 30 pieces at each of four target lengths: 2,048, 4,096, 8,192, and 12,288 tokens, using checkpoint 46,000 with temperature 0.9. We evaluated the generation process along three dimensions:

\subsubsection{Musical Output Properties}
We measured the number of musical bars, the number of individual notes, and the token density per bar (tokens per bar) in the generated output to assess whether the model produces musically reasonable content at different lengths.

\subsubsection{Generation Performance}
We recorded the wall-clock generation time and computed the throughput in tokens per second to characterize the computational efficiency of the recurrent generator.

\subsubsection{Token Grammar Quality}
We measured the grammar error rate --- the percentage of generated tokens that violate the REMIGEN grammar (before filtering) --- to assess how well the model maintains grammatical coherence as sequence length increases.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsubsection{Perplexity and Sequence Length Extrapolation}
A key advantage of the xLSTM architecture is its ability to extrapolate beyond the training context length when using the recurrent formulation. To evaluate this capability, we measured perplexity on the validation set at various sequence lengths, including lengths exceeding the training context of 4,096 tokens.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/ppl/seq_length_vs_ppl.pdf}
    \caption{Perplexity as a function of context length for checkpoint 46,000. The dashed red line indicates the training context length (4,096) and the shaded region marks the extrapolation regime.}
    \label{fig:perplexity_vs_seqlen}
\end{figure}

As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:perplexity_vs_seqlen}, perplexity decreases as context length increases up to and slightly beyond the training context length of 4,096. The model achieves its lowest perplexity of 1.65 at a context length of 5,120, which is beyond the training context. Perplexity then gradually increases to 1.89 at context length 10,240. This demonstrates that the xLSTM model can effectively utilize context beyond its training length, though with some degradation at significantly longer contexts.

Table~\ref{tab:ppl_comparison} places our xLSTM perplexity values alongside those reported by Yu et al.\ \cite{yu2022museformer} for several Transformer-based models evaluated on the same dataset (LMD) with the same encoding (REMIGEN). Note that a direct comparison should be interpreted with caution: the models differ in architecture, parameter count, and dataset preprocessing. Museformer uses 29,940 files filtered to 4/4 time with six normalized instruments; our preprocessed dataset comprises 24,402 of those files after independently applying the same filtering and normalisation steps. Despite these differences, the comparison provides useful context. Our xLSTM model achieves competitive perplexity at shorter context lengths (1.80 at 1,024 tokens) and maintains reasonable performance in the extrapolation regime (1.89 at 10,240), though it does not match Museformer's strong results at longer contexts, where its structure-aware attention mechanism appears to provide a clear advantage.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{xLSTM as an Architecture for Music Generation}

Our results demonstrate that xLSTM is a viable architecture for symbolic music generation, with several notable properties. The model successfully learns the REMIGEN token grammar and produces musically meaningful output (as evidenced by the proportional scaling of bars and notes with sequence length, and the stable token density per bar). The recurrent formulation provides practical advantages that are particularly relevant for music generation: constant memory usage and linear-time generation make it feasible to generate long pieces that would be impractical with the naive parallel inference approach.

\subsection{Recurrent vs. Parallel Inference}

The inference bottleneck we identified in the Helibrunna framework highlights an important practical consideration when working with xLSTM models. While the parallel and recurrent formulations are mathematically equivalent, the choice of which formulation to use at inference time has dramatic consequences for performance. Our recurrent generator achieved speedups ranging from 2.8$\times$ at 100 tokens to over 25$\times$ at 12,000 tokens (projected), with the advantage growing as sequence length increases. This finding is relevant beyond music generation --- any application using xLSTM for autoregressive generation should use the recurrent formulation to avoid unnecessary quadratic overhead.

\subsection{Extrapolation Capabilities and Limitations}

The model demonstrates meaningful extrapolation beyond its training context length of 4,096 tokens, maintaining low perplexity and near-zero grammar errors up to approximately 5,000 tokens. Beyond this range, we observe a gradual increase in both perplexity and grammar error rate, though the model continues to produce decodable music even at 12,288 tokens (3$\times$ the training context). This extrapolation ability is consistent with the xLSTM paper's findings for language modeling \cite{beck2024xlstm}, where models trained at context length 2,048 maintained low perplexity up to 16,384 tokens.

\subsection{Comparison with Transformer-Based Approaches}

The xLSTM architecture offers a fundamentally different trade-off compared to Transformer-based music generation systems like Museformer \cite{yu2022museformer}. Museformer addresses the long-sequence challenge by introducing specialized fine- and coarse-grained attention patterns, achieving effective complexity between linear and quadratic and successfully enabling full-song generation with high structural quality. The xLSTM takes a different approach: its recurrent state naturally compresses the full sequence history into a fixed-size representation, providing constant-time and constant-memory generation per token without requiring any specialized attention mechanisms. However, this compressed representation may also limit the model's ability to selectively attend to specific earlier passages --- a capability that Museformer's fine-grained attention explicitly provides for structure-related bars. The two architectures thus represent complementary approaches to the long-form generation problem, each with distinct strengths.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have presented a study on the application of xLSTM to symbolic music generation, focusing on efficient long-form generation and evaluation of the generation process. Our key findings are:

First, the xLSTM architecture can be successfully trained on large-scale symbolic music data (611.6M tokens from the Lakh MIDI Dataset) using the Helibrunna framework, demonstrating sequence length extrapolation beyond the training context.

Second, we identified and resolved a critical inference bottleneck by implementing a recurrent-state generator that reduces the computational complexity of generation from $O(N^2)$ to $O(N)$, enabling generation of long sequences that were previously infeasible due to time and memory constraints.

Third, our evaluation across multiple sequence lengths shows that the model produces musically proportionate output with stable token density and maintains constant generation throughput regardless of sequence length, though grammar adherence degrades gradually as sequences extend beyond the training context.

These results suggest that modern recurrent architectures like xLSTM offer a promising direction for long-form symbolic music generation, providing natural advantages in computational efficiency and memory usage that complement the capabilities of Transformer-based approaches.

\subsection{Future Work}

Future directions include training with longer context lengths to improve extrapolation, exploring different xLSTM configurations (number of layers, embedding dimensions, sLSTM/mLSTM ratios), investigating constrained decoding strategies to reduce grammar errors at long sequence lengths, and conducting more extensive comparisons with Transformer-based models including Museformer on identical datasets and evaluation metrics.
